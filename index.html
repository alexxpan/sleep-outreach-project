A Sleep Outreach Project

Humans spend approximately one third of their lives asleep. This site is dedicated to informing the public about why sleep is so special, how sleep affects our lives, and why you're probably sleep deprived.

The Basics


There are two types of sleep: non-rapid eye movement sleep (NREM), and rapid eye movement sleep (REM).

NREM

Within NREM sleep, there are 4 different stages of sleep. Stages 3 and 4 of NREM are referred to as slow wave sleep (SWS).

REM

During REM sleep, your body becomes paralyzed and you experience sporadic eye movements. Dreams usually occur in REM sleep.

Circadian Rhythm

Your wake/sleep cycle, or 'circadian rhythm', is controlled by an innate biological clock. The superchaismatic nucleus determines when to secret melatonin, a sleep hormone.

Sleep Cycles

Throughout the night, you cycle through the stages of NREM and REM every 90 minutes. The four stages of NREM happen first, and REM is the final stage of sleep.

Sleep and caffeine
Caffeine blocks the signal of adenosine, the chemical that causes you to feel sleepy. When caffeine wears off, you get hit with all of the adenosine that was building up â€” which is why you crash.
Sleep and Creativity
REM sleep, especially, has been associated with creativity. Paul McCartney claimed that he came up with many of his songs in his sleep. In fact, Mendeleev reported that he even invented the periodic table in a dream.
Sleep and Memory
Sleep affects memory with a complex blend of different types of sleep interacting with different stages of memory. Both declarative (fact-based) and procedural memory have been proven to be improved by sleeping.
Measuring Sleep
The gold standard of measuring sleep is physiology. There are 3 tools we use to measure a person's sleep: EEG (brain activity), EOG (eye activity), and EMG (muscle activity).
Afternoon Naps
There is scientific evidence that suggests that humans were meant to take naps during the afternoon. The 'postprandial dip' refers to a period during the afternoon where we experience a slump in our energy.
Sleep and Alcohol
Alcohol is proven to suppress REM sleep, and can cause cognitive deficits with lasting effects. Drinking before bed can prevent memories from being consolidated, even several nights after you learn new information.

The Dangers


The average person should sleep around 9 hours a night. The average college student probably gets about two thirds of that. Not getting enough sleep can have potentially devastating effects, both short term and long term. Here are just a few reasons why you should clock in your 9 (not 8!) hours every night.



Immune Function

Lack of sleep has drastic negative effects on your bodyâ€™s immune system. After just 4 hours of sleep reduction, there is a 70% reduction in cancer-defense (NK) cells.


Metabolism

Sleep loss is associated with impaired glucose tolerance. After just a week of sleeping for 4 hours a night, patients had glucose tolerance levels similar to those were significantly at risk of diabetes.


Drowsy Driving

After 16 hours of wakefulness, your performance on alertness tasks degrades below the performance of those who are legally drunk. Falling asleep at the wheel is the cause of more than 100,000 accidents per year.


Professional Impact

One in five medical residents will seriously harm a patient due to a fatigue related error. One in twenty medical residents will kill a patient due to a fatigue related error.


Emotional Health

REM sleep is associated with emotional and mental stability. Lack of sleep can cause overactive emotions, and may even lead to permanent psychological damage in severe cases.
